Professional Persona Arc

From Self-Perception
to Reputation

Through BCOM 314's Professional Persona Arc — a structured process involving a Self-Perception Inventory (an assessment of how I see myself), a 360° feedback cycle from peers and mentors, and a Professional Reputation Survey — I've developed a data-informed picture of who I am as a professional and how others experience working with me.

Professional Persona Video
01

Core Values

Competitiveness, connection, and creativity form the backbone of how I operate. My background in hockey instilled a drive to win — but never at the expense of the team. These values consistently surface in peer feedback as reliability and high energy.

02

Communication Style

My Self-Perception Inventory revealed a high-assertive, relationship-oriented communication style. I tend to lead conversations with energy and optimism, though I've learned to balance this with active listening to ensure every voice in the room is heard.

03

Growth Mindset

Feedback from teammates and instructors consistently flagged my openness to learning as a strength. I've intentionally leaned into this — treating every internship, assignment, and tough conversation as a rung on the ladder rather than a verdict on my ability.

04

Warmth & Competence

Research shows that professionals are judged on both warmth and competence. Survey respondents described me as approachable and collaborative (warmth) while recognizing sharp analytical instincts (competence) — a balance I continue to cultivate.

05

Intention vs. Impact

One key lesson from this semester: my enthusiasm can sometimes outpace my precision. While I intend to energize a room, the impact can occasionally read as unfocused. I'm actively working on channeling that energy into structured, deliberate delivery.

06

Going Forward

I'm entering the finance industry knowing exactly who I am and who I want to become. The gap between my self-perception and reputation is small — and closing. My goal is to build a career where authenticity and analytical rigor reinforce each other.

The Alignment Question

The central question of this arc was: To what extent does my self-perception align with how others perceive me? The short answer: more than I expected, with nuance where it counts most.

The adjectives I used to describe myself — driven, sociable, analytical, enthusiastic — largely mirrored what survey respondents and teammates reflected back. Where divergence emerged was in tempo: I move fast and assume others are tracking; feedback reminded me that thoroughness is just as compelling to a finance audience as velocity.

This insight directly shapes how I'll present myself going forward: leading with structure before enthusiasm, and letting the numbers do the talking first. A career in investment banking demands precision as much as personality — and I'm building both.
